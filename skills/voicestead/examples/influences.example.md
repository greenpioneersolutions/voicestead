# Influences — example (Sam, an engineering manager)

_A worked example so you can see what a filled-in influences file looks like — one Influence
Profile, one studied Influence Card. Delete this and build your own; it's illustrative, not a
default to imitate. The card below was researched with web tools, so it's marked Verified:
researched._

## Influence Profile
Admires:            George Orwell — "he makes hard things plain without dumbing them down. You never catch him hiding."
Reaching toward:    the reader finishes and thinks *that was obvious*, when it wasn't obvious until Sam wrote it down.
Anti-influences:    corporate memo fog — the kind that says "leverage our synergies" and means nothing.
Inherited voices:   a first manager who answered every long question with one short sentence.

## Influence Cards

### George Orwell — the essays, especially "Politics and the English Language" (1946)
Why it's here:      "His rules are basically this skill in six lines. He caught the fog problem in 1946 and named it."
Moves to borrow:
  1. Cut every word that does no work. Orwell's rule: "If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out." After drafting, read each sentence and delete any word whose removal doesn't change the meaning — then check whether the sentence got sharper.
  2. Prefer the short, concrete word to the long abstract one. Where a plain everyday word exists, take it over the Latinate or jargon version — "use," not "utilize"; "before," not "prior to." If you wouldn't say it out loud to one person, don't write it.
  3. Default to the active voice and name who acted. "The decision was reached" hides the actor; "we decided" doesn't. Reach for passive only when the doer is genuinely unknown or beside the point.
Reach for it when:  a draft has gone soft or evasive — status updates, postmortems, anything where fog is creeping in and you need to sound like you actually mean it.
Not for:            warmth-first writing (condolence, celebration, a thank-you). Orwell's clarity can read cold; lead with the voice profile there and let this card sit out.
Fragment:           "The great enemy of clear language is insincerity."
Verified:           researched

---

**Deliberate-device note.** Orwell states most of his rules as flat anaphora — "Never use a metaphor… Never use a long word… Never use the passive…" — parallel repetition, one clause after another in the same shape. That brushes against the skill's rhythm tells: metronomic parallelism and the reflexive rule-of-three. It works for Orwell because it's a mnemonic rulebook, deployed once and structurally, not sprinkled as filler. Borrow the pattern the way inspiration.md licenses it — a deliberate device, used sparingly and on purpose, never a tic. And note his sixth rule is itself the escape hatch: "Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous." That's the same moderation rule this skill runs on — context always wins over the checklist.
